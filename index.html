Project 1: House Price Prediction

This project predicts Miami house prices using regression models and a dataset of house features. It is my first machine learning project, applying techniques like exploratory data analysis, outlier removal, and linear regression, along with new methods like data splitting and decision trees. The correlation analysis reveals that geographical features (LATITUDE, LONGITUDE) significantly impact property values, with properties closer to the coast or central areas fetching higher prices. Size-related features (LND_SQFOOT, TOT_LVG_AREA) strongly correlate with higher sale prices, indicating larger properties are more expensive. Special features (SPEC_FEAT_VAL) vary in impact, while distance-related features (RAIL_DIST, OCEAN_DIST, WATER_DIST, CNTR_DIST, SUBCNTR_DI, HWY_DIST) highlight the importance of accessibility and proximity to key locations. Property age shows a potential negative correlation, as older properties may depreciate. Demographic feature avno60plus affects demand in areas with significant populations over 60, and seasonal trends (month_sold) show price fluctuations throughout the year. Structure_quality is crucial, with higher quality structures commanding higher prices.

Project 2: Titanic Survival Prediction

This project uses various machine learning algorithms to predict the survival of Titanic passengers based on features such as age, gender, and ticket class. As my first Kaggle project, I learned new techniques for creating variables, and explored more models and classifiers, including the Gradient Booster, through participation in the Kaggle community. The survival correlation analysis of the Titanic dataset reveals key insights. Passengers from Cherbourg had the highest survival rate (55.36%), while first-class passengers had a higher survival rate (62.96%) compared to second and third class. Females had a significantly higher survival rate (74.20%) than males (18.89%). Passengers with 2-4 family members had higher survival rates, peaking at 4 members (72.41%), while very large families had lower survival rates. Younger passengers had the highest survival rate (57.35%). Higher fare groups correlated with higher survival rates, with the highest fare group at 59.72%. Passengers traveling with companions had a higher survival rate (50.57%) than those traveling alone (30.35%). Overall, survival was strongly influenced by embarkation point, class, gender, family size, age, fare paid, travel companions, and social status.

Project 3: Health Insurance Project

This dataset pertains to an insurance company aiming to predict if past policyholders will purchase vehicle insurance. This project introduces more complex techniques, including the XGB Booster classifier. Analysis reveals that gender has a minor impact on survival, with females slightly outperforming males. Survival rates generally increase with age, and those with a driving license have a higher survival rate compared to those without. Survival rates vary widely by region, with Region_Code 19 having the highest and Region_Code 40 the lowest. Previously insured individuals show much lower survival rates than those who were not insured before. Older vehicles (>2 years) have the highest survival rates, while newer ones (less than 1 year) have the lowest. Damaged vehicles also have a higher survival rate compared to undamaged ones. Survival rates differ by annual premium and policy sales channel, with some channels and premium ranges showing notable differences. Age groups 5 and 6 have the highest survival rates. Overall, survival is influenced by age, driving license status, vehicle condition, and region.

Project 4: MNIST Digit Recognition

This project involves recognizing handwritten digits using a neural network trained on the MNIST dataset, which requires different techniques such as sequential models and epoch-based training. The dataset comprises grayscale images of digits from 0 to 9, available in train.csv and test.csv. Each image is 28x28 pixels, resulting in 784 pixel values per image, with values ranging from 0 (white) to 255 (black). In train.csv, there are 785 columns: the first column is the digit label, and the remaining 784 columns represent pixel values. Each pixel column is named pixelx, where x ranges from 0 to 783. To locate a pixel, decompose x into its row (i) and column (j) within the 28x28 grid. For instance, pixel31 corresponds to the pixel in the fourth column and second row of the grid.
